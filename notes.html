The observations on this page are not formally derived from the double cube's axiomatic core. The remainder

γ — what always stays behind.

When two regimes negotiate, something is always left over. The negotiation is never perfect. This is not a flaw — it is a structural guarantee.

Euler's constant γ = 0.57722... is that remainder.

Reading γ as a geometric address in the double cube: 0 sits below one full cycle. 5 points to the √5 corner — the full cube must be included. 7 via the saddle point. 2×2 spans two cubes. The digits describe a position in the system before we even do the mathematics.

And the mathematics is striking:

γ · √3 = 0.57722 × 1.73205 ≈ 1.00000

Euler's constant, scaled by the face diagonal of the cube, returns approximately one full cycle — with a deviation of just 0.023%. γ is not obviously a geometric constant. It measures the gap between discrete summation and continuous integration — the harmonic series versus the natural logarithm. That this gap should approximate the reciprocal of √3 — a pure geometric property of cubic space — is either a deep structural connection or a remarkable numerical coincidence. It has not been formally explained.

Open question

Is γ · √3 = 1 exact, or merely approximate? If exact, it would connect the discrete–continuous divide directly to cubic geometry. If approximate, the deviation of 0.023% may itself carry geometric meaning — possibly related to the overlap δ = 14/327 in the periodic table construction.

e — the rotation that completes the cycle

If γ is the remainder that always stays behind, e is the rotation that carries the system forward into the next cycle.

Euler's number e = 2.71828... reads in two layers.

The integer — 2 — is two complete cubic cycles. Exact. No remainder. The structure is satisfied.

The decimals — 0.71828... — describe a path: 7 → 1 → 8 → 2. In the double cube, these are not arbitrary digits. They suggest a journey through the system's own geometry — from the saddle point √7, via the unit √1, through the triple cube's threshold √8 = 2√2, and back to the ground tone √2 where the rotation is complete.

Now imagine placing e on a circle of radius √7. The rotation angle is 44.09°. Almost 45° — and 45° is the angle of √2, the double cube's own ground tone. e lands just short of where the double cube naturally rests. That gap is e's own geometric signature in the system.

e does not belong to the double cube. It belongs to the transition — the rotation that carries the system from the double cube into the triple cube. e^(iπ) = −1 is not halfway through something arbitrary. It is one step in a two-step cycle where the triple cube is the natural endpoint.

Place e − 2 on a circle of radius √7:

Re = √7 · (e − 2) Im = √7 · sin(arccos(e − 2))

Then:

Re² + Im² = 7

This is an exact algebraic identity — it follows directly from sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, scaled by √7. No numerical approximation. It holds for any value of e.

The rotation angle produces a second exact identity:

sin²(θ) = (3 − e)(e − 1)

e sits between 1 and 3 — the cube's own boundary values. The gap from each boundary is e's precise position in the system.

The triangle (√2, √7, √8) that this rotation generates:

√8² − √7² = 1 · √8² − √2² = 6 · √7² − √2² = 5

Combined with its own sides, this triangle covers the entire double cube.

γ and e — a family

γ measures what cannot be resolved inside the period. e rotates through what remains and completes it. They are two sides of the same fundamental tension — as the double cube's two regimes are two sides of the same structure.

The normalisation factor that connects them:

γ · √3 ≈ 1 e · γ · √3 ≈ e (deviation 0.023%)

The same factor — γ · √3 — returns both to themselves. Whether this is structural or coincidental is the open question.

Convergences

Three values, one threshold.

The percolation threshold 1/√3 = 0.57735... appears to attract values from independent directions:

1/√3 = 0.57735... (geometric — reciprocal body diagonal) γ = 0.57722... (analytic — Euler–Mascheroni constant) 49/85 = 0.57647... (from the periodic table — counting argument)

The ratio 49/85 counts: (Z_Yb − Z_Sc) / (Z_Rn − Z_H) — the span of the d- and f-blocks divided by the full span of the table through period 6. No external constants. A pure counting statement about element positions.

All three differ from each other by less than 0.00088. Whether this is a structural convergence or a numerical coincidence has not been determined.

Open question

Does the double cube's geometry — specifically the saddle point 7/4 and the percolation interval 1/12 — provide a geometric explanation for why the Euler–Mascheroni constant and the element-count ratio converge near 1/√3?

A hypothesis

Geometric addresses in the double cube.

The digit-reading of γ = 0.57722... as a geometric address in the double cube — 0 below one cycle, 5 at the √5 corner, 7 via the saddle point, 2×2 spanning two cubes — is an illustration, not a proof. But it raises a question worth asking formally:

Are the decimal expansions of transcendental constants random, or do they encode geometric positions in a structured space? If the double cube is the minimal structure that couples periodicity to Euclidean geometry, then constants that arise at the boundary between discrete and continuous mathematics — γ, e, π, α — might have geometric addresses in this structure.

This is a falsifiable hypothesis: for each constant, there should exist a geometric path in the double cube's square-root system that reproduces the decimal structure — not numerically by chance, but by structural necessity. This has been demonstrated suggestively for γ and e. It has not been systematically tested for π or α.
