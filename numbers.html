How space may coordinate the values of geometric and accumulating numbers.

Every number has two faces: the geometric, structural and the cumulative, linear — we use the same numbers to describe both coordinates and sums. These two very different domains, normally treated as separate phenomena, are calculable with the same system consisting of 10 numbers in infinite combinations: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 0.

Although the system has served us well since the beginning of calculation, the question remains:

Are the two-faced properties of numbers related in a certain way?
Is there a geometry that coordinates them?
The structure

Meet the double cube.

The structure that corners the numbers.

The double cube has been used since ancient times to explain the geometric properties of numbers. It is a visual and educational masterpiece. But it might be more than that.

A single cube shows us √1, √2 and √3 — edge, face diagonal, space diagonal. Add a second cube sharing a face, and something interesting happens. √4 and √6 are natural doublings of what we already know. But you see — the second cube is not just a repetition of the single cube, and √5 is the snitch telling us that something new emerges when the two seemingly similar structures are placed side by side.

√5

The dynamic connector. Traverses both cubes. Cannot exist in a single cube alone. Tells us the double cube also represents something cyclical.

√6

A natural doubling of √3. Structural. Predictable. The double cube's space diagonal — still playing by the rules from the first cube.

Missing in action

Viewing the double cube reveals something peculiar as you digest these facts: adding a third cube would not create the same magic. You won't get another √5 factor, but instead end up with a cleaner, more easily divisible √10, echoing √2.

And before we even go so far as to add a third cube — where is √7?

The cube effectively codes √1 to √3, and adding a tessellating cube expands this to √1 to √6. These are all natural cubic proportions. √7 is a combinator: √(2² + (√3)²) = √7. It is constructable over both regimes at the same time as it is not part of the convex outer shell of the double cube. The tangent from the √7 sphere to the √6 surface is √(7−6) = √1 = 1 exactly.

The double cube, historically, has not been regarded as a system of numbers. That may be why the question about which square roots it produces — and more pressing, why √7 sits outside the system — seems not to have been asked formally.

A proof in three layers

Layer 1 — Exhaustive check. All 12 corners of the 2×1×1 double cube have coordinates (x,y,z) where x∈{0,1,2}, y∈{0,1}, z∈{0,1}. The squared distances x²+y²+z² from the origin — and between all corner pairs — produce exactly the set {0,1,2,3,4,5,6}. No pair of corners produces a squared distance of 7. This is not an axiom. It is a finite, exhaustive proof.

Layer 2 — Algebra (Wantzel, 1837). ∛2 is irrational and can therefore never be a corner point in an integer lattice. This was proved in the resolution of the Delian problem — the classical impossibility of doubling a cube by compass and straightedge alone.

Layer 3 — The connection. The double cube is closed at n=6 for corner distances. 7 is the first integer that breaks out of this corner structure. The Delian problem requires ∛2 — which is equally absent as a corner point. Both are expressions of the same structural limitation: the system cannot contain them as points, but √7 defines the spherical boundary where the system opens. The Delian problem appears to operate in the same gap — this connection deserves formal attention.

What follows. √7 is not arbitrarily placed outside the double cube. It is the first and only value that the system's own geometry excludes — and simultaneously requires, as the combinator that binds the two regimes. Its curvature is not assumed. It is a structural consequence of the lattice.

Layer 4 — An exact identity connecting √7 and ∛2

∛2 and √7 are not independent of each other. √7 can be written as ∛2 plus a gap:

√7 = ∛2 + (√7 − ∛2)

Squaring both sides via the binomial expansion gives an exact identity:

(∛2)² + 2·∛2·(√7 − ∛2) + (√7 − ∛2)² = 7

Written structurally, this decomposes 7 into three exact terms:

∛4 — the Delian constant squared 2·∛2·(√7−∛2) — the transition term (√7−∛2)² — the gap squared ───────────────── Sum = 7 ✓ (exact)

The gap itself is √7 − ∛2 = 1.38583... — the precise distance between the double cube's spherical boundary and the Delian constant. Both are absent from the integer lattice of the double cube, for different reasons: √7 because no corner pair produces a squared distance of 7, and ∛2 because it is irrational (Wantzel). Yet they are connected by this exact identity.

Whether this connection is structural — pointing toward a deeper relationship between cubic topology, spherical geometry and the Delian problem — or whether it is a consequence of binomial algebra alone, remains an open question. The identity itself is exact and requires no assumptions. Its interpretation does.

The conjecture is this: the same gap that separates ∛2 from √7 in the double cube may reappear in the periodic table at the boundary between the s-block and the d-block, and in the percolation interval 1/12 that connects Kleibers law to the double cube's diagonal structure. If so, the Delian problem is not a historical curiosity — it is a geometric signature of the same system.

Here's where the real magic starts — if we take this question seriously, we may propose one major assumption: the double cube, bound by √5 as its dynamic connector, points towards a closed system. √7 does not escape — it curves. The expression (√7/2)² = 7/4 is the geometric signature of this curvature, hinting at this as a candidate saddle point at the interface between cubic and spherical symmetry in the double cube — the point where the two geometries can negotiate without either dominating. √7's value also places it firmly in the second cube, close to √6. As the numbers and examples will show, this is more than a fair assumption.

Let's negotiate

√7, given the assumption, is the one that will not be squared into a corner. Instead it literally wants to play ball. The √7 diagonal traverses space and ends up roaming the cubes. It's the negotiator between the two regimes.

The two binders

So here we are.

We have two separate cubes and two numbers that bind them together: √5 and √7.

√5

The dynamic one. It traverses the two cubes and tells us that they are not only a structure, but half a cycle.

√7

The odd one. It tells us that negotiating the cycle involves more than the corners. It needs the space. And it does so by snitching about the fact that the double cube hides a spherical element.

The negotiating parts

We now see our numbers in their true two-faced properties: the cyclic/periodic and the structural/geometric.

And what is even deeper: do we also see the possible shape of a hypothesis? That every number carries both these properties at the same time, and that factorisation uncovers its geometrical identity, while the cyclical uncovers a cumulative position?

The modest double cube may turn out to deliver a great punch for its size and shape. Assuming √7 is contained in the construction, it gives us an operating system where these two domains meet.

The spherical element that the √7 tangent revealed might not just be a geometric curiosity. It has a precise half expression given its place in the double cube, and names the candidate saddle point in the system, as mentioned before:

(√7 / 2)² = 7/4 = 1.75

Simply put: √7 is the geometry. 7/4 is what it does.

This number, 7/4 = 1.75, is the double cube's way of telling us that the cycle it hides negotiates the geometrical length of √4 to 1.75 when translated into cyclic motion. And when we change the two-faced numbers from geometric value to cumulative value, we discover that the geometric double cube possibly hides a triple-based spherical motion — a full period.

The way to spot this goes through the shared face between the cubes: each cube has 8 corners, but sharing a face leaves 12. So the total length of the double cube, divided by 7, gives us a spatial hint through its corners:

7/4 × 12 = 21 = 3 × 7

The full negotiating space is 21 = 3 × 7. The threshold 7/4 and the corners of the double cube speak the same language.

Proposition: The dynamic side of the double cube

Definition 1. Let a double cube be two unit cubes linked along one shared face. The double cube has 2×8 − 4 = 12 corners, where 4 shared corners are subtracted.

Definition 2. Let the saddle point S be defined as S = (√7/2)² = 7/4, where √7 is the combinator — the smallest square root not contained in the double cube's convex shell, but constructable as √(2² + (√3)²).

Lemma. For n linked unit cubes, the number of corners is 4(n+1), and the product with the saddle point is:

4(n+1) · 7/4 = 7(n+1)

This is an exact algebraic identity.

Corollary for n = 2 (the double cube):

12 · 7/4 = 21 = 3 × 7

Interpretation. The factor 3 corresponds to the spherical content of the double cube: one full sphere in the centre and two half-spheres at the ends — the structure ½ + 1 + ½. The factor 7 is the combinator. The product 21 is where the cubic topology and the spherical geometry meet as an exact integer.

Four independent convergences on 21

1. √21 = √(1² + 2² + 4²) — the space diagonal of a 1×2×4 rectangle, where 1, 2, 4 are cubic powers of the base edge.

2. 7π/21 = π/3 = 60° exactly — the area of the saddle point sphere (4π · 7/4 = 7π) divided by 21 gives the base angle of hexagonal crystallography.

3. 21 × γ × √3 = 20.995 ≈ 21 — the same normalisation principle as γ · √3 ≈ 1 for e and α⁻¹.

4. Sc is element 21 in the periodic table — the first element requiring a d-orbital, where the structural shift from the spherical s-regime to the cubic d-regime occurs.

Conclusion

The dynamic side of the double cube is established by the fact that the topological corner count, weighted by the combinator's saddle point, generates the spherical segment count and the combinator as a necessary product. The argument is internally consistent, algebraically exact in its core, and converges with four independent observations.

Remaining weakness

The weighting of corners by the saddle point 7/4 is geometrically motivated but not formally proved — an explicit axiom connecting cubic topology with spherical weighting is still missing.

Synthesis

A closing remark and a new perspective.

The double cube's essence is the tension between two boundary conditions: 2/3 and 3/4.

2/3 is the ratio seen from the cumulative side: 2 periods across 3 spheres — the structure ½ + 1 + ½ distributed along the dynamic axis.

3/4 is the saddle point's distance from unity: 7/4 − 1 = 3/4. It is not chosen — it follows from the combinator's own position in the system.

These two boundary conditions define the negotiating space of the double cube. Their difference is the remainder — the percolation interval:

3/4 − 2/3 = 1/12

1/12 is not a correction factor. It is the structural gap between the two faces of the system — the space where the negotiation actually happens.

The threshold

The third cube — a new string length.

While the double cube is a self-contained system identifying both structural and cyclical behaviour, some constants and numbers need a third cube to be explained geometrically.

To explain this, let's use an analogy: a double cube is structurally similar to a string of a certain length — described dimensionlessly as √4. √7, via the 7/4 factor, defines the string's minimal harmonic partial, marking the limit of its structural coherence. The 7th partial is disharmonic at the harmonic level — it lives within the string, but marks the threshold of resolution. The geometry can no longer resolve itself cleanly here. The string does not break. It seeks another level — a new string length.

That new string length is the triple cube. Not a new geometry. The same system, in the next octave.

This is where e enters. Not as a number that belongs to the double cube — but as the rotation constant for the transition itself.

The remainder

γ — what always stays behind.

When two regimes negotiate, something is always left over. The negotiation is never perfect. This is not a flaw — it is a structural guarantee.

Euler's constant γ = 0.57722... is that remainder.

Reading γ as a geometric address in the double cube: 0 sits below one full cycle. 5 points to the √5 corner — the full cube must be included. 7 via the saddle point. 2×2 spans two cubes. The digits describe a position in the system before we even do the mathematics.

And the mathematics confirms it:

γ · √3 = 0.57722 × 1.73205 ≈ 1.00000

Euler's constant, scaled by the face diagonal of the cube, returns exactly one full cycle. γ is not a mysterious analytic constant. It is the remainder when the cumulative regime meets the geometric — structurally guaranteed by the incommensurability between the two. It lives inside the period, measuring what cannot be resolved.

e — the rotation that completes the cycle

If γ is the remainder that always stays behind, e is the rotation that carries the system forward into the next cycle.

Euler's number e = 2.71828... reads in two layers.

The integer — 2 — is two complete cubic cycles. Exact. No remainder. The structure is satisfied.

The decimals — 0.71828... — are something else entirely. They describe a path: 7 → 1 → 8 → 2. In the double cube, these are not arbitrary digits. They are a journey through the system's own geometry — from the saddle point √7, via the unit √1, through the triple cube's threshold √8 = 2√2, and back to the ground tone √2 where the rotation is complete.

Now imagine placing e on a circle. Not the whole number — just the decimal part, e − 2 = 0.71828... The natural radius for this circle, given where the path has taken us, is √7.

The rotation angle is 44.09°. Almost 45°. And 45° is the angle of √2 — the double cube's own ground tone. e lands just short of where the double cube naturally rests. That gap — less than one degree — is e's own geometric signature in the system.

e does not belong to the double cube. It belongs to the transition — the rotation that carries the system from the double cube into the triple cube. e^(iπ) = −1 is not halfway through something arbitrary. It is one step in a two-step cycle where the triple cube is the natural endpoint.

Place e − 2 on a circle of radius √7:

Re = √7 · (e − 2) Im = √7 · sin(arccos(e − 2))

Then:

Re² + Im² = 7

This is an exact algebraic identity — it follows directly from sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, scaled by √7. No numerical approximation. It holds for any value of e.

The rotation angle produces a second exact identity:

sin²(θ) = (3 − e)(e − 1)

e sits between 1 and 3 — the cube's own boundary values. The gap from each boundary is not arbitrary. It is e's precise position in the system.

The triangle (√2, √7, √8) that this rotation generates is complete:

√8² − √7² = 1 · √8² − √2² = 6 · √7² − √2² = 5

Combined with its own sides, this triangle covers the entire double cube.

γ and e — a family

γ measures what cannot be resolved inside the period. e rotates through what remains and completes it. They are two sides of the same fundamental tension — as the double cube's two regimes are two sides of the same structure.

The normalisation factor that connects them:
