A geometric exploration

Negotiating numbers.

How space may coordinate the values of geometric and accumulating numbers.

Every number has two faces: the geometric/structural and the cumulative/linear — we use the same numbers to describe both coordinates and sums. These two very different domains, normally treated as separate phenomena, are calculable with the same system consisting of 10 numbers in infinite combinations: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 0.

Although the system has served us well since the beginning of calculation, the question remains:

Are the two-faced properties of numbers related in a certain way?
Is there a geometry that coordinates them?
The structure

Meet the double cube.

The structure that corners the numbers.

The double cube has been used since ancient times to explain the geometric properties of numbers. It is a visual and educational masterpiece. But it might be more than that.

A single cube shows us √1, √2 and √3 — edge, face diagonal, space diagonal. Add a second cube sharing a face, and something interesting happens. √4 and √6 are natural doublings of what we already know. But you see — the second cube is not just a repetition of the single cube, and √5 is the snitch telling us that something new emerges when the two seemingly similar structures are placed side by side.

√4 and √6

Natural doublings of √2 and √3. Structural. Predictable. The double cube's space diagonals — still playing by the rules from the first cube.

√5

The dynamic connector. Traverses both cubes. Cannot exist in a single cube alone. Tells us the double cube also represents something cyclical. And it carries a familiar constant: φ = (1 + √5)/2 — the golden ratio, already present in the system, not imported from outside.

The double cube is the minimal integer lattice construction that contains φ as an internal geometric ratio. The right triangle with sides 1, 2 and √5 is inscribed in the double cube's own face — and φ = (1 + √5)/2 is the ratio of these three lengths: the short side plus the hypotenuse, divided by the long side.

The one missing in action

When studying the double cube, something else peculiar turns up: adding a third cube will not create the same magic. You won't get another √5 factor, but instead end up with a cleaner, more easily divisible √10 corner, echoing √2.

And before we even go so far as to add a third cube — where exactly is √7?

The cube effectively codes √1 to √3, and adding a tessellating cube expands this to √1 to √6. These are all natural cubic proportions. √7 is a combinator: √(2² + (√3)²) = √7. It is constructable over both regimes at the same time as it is not part of the convex outer shell of the double cube. The tangent from the √7 sphere to the √6 surface is √(7−6) = √1 = 1 exactly.

The double cube, historically, has not been regarded as a system of numbers. That may be why the question about which square roots it produces — and more pressing, why √7 sits outside the system — seems not to have been asked formally.

Layer 1 — Exhaustive verification

All 12 corners of the 2×1×1 double cube have coordinates (x,y,z) where x∈{0,1,2}, y∈{0,1}, z∈{0,1}. The squared distances x²+y²+z² from the origin — and between all corner pairs — produce exactly the set {0,1,2,3,4,5,6}. No pair of corners produces a squared distance of 7. This is not an axiom. It is a finite, exhaustive proof.

Layer 2 — Legendre's three-square theorem (1798)

A positive integer n can be written as the sum of three squares of integers if and only if n is not of the form 4ᵃ(8b+7). The number 7 satisfies this exactly: 7 = 4⁰(8·0+7). It is the simplest and smallest member of this infinite family of forbidden values — {7, 15, 23, 28, 31, ...}. This is a proved theorem. √7 is absent from the double cube's lattice by mathematical necessity.

Layer 3 — Lagrange's four-square theorem

To realise 7 as a sum of integer squares, four terms are required: 7 = 2²+1²+1²+1². Geometrically, this means √7 requires a fourth spatial dimension to exist as a lattice point. In the context of the double cube, this fourth degree of freedom is interpreted as periodicity: a temporal or cyclic dimension that complements the three spatial ones.

Layer 3b — Geometric proof of 7/4 as the saddle point

Draw a circle of radius √7 centred at the origin. Place an external point P = (4, 0) — the square of the double cube's length (2² = 4). The tangent length is exact:

|PT| = √(16 − 7) = √9 = 3

The x-coordinate of the tangent point:

Tₓ = r² / |OP| = 7 / 4 = 1.75

The saddle point is not placed by assumption — it is where the tangent from the double cube's squared length meets the √7 sphere.

Layer 4 — An exact identity connecting √7 and ∛2

√7 = ∛2 + (√7 − ∛2). Squaring gives:

(∛2)² + 2·∛2·(√7 − ∛2) + (√7 − ∛2)² = 7

∛4 (Delian²) + transition term + gap² = 7, exactly. The gap √7 − ∛2 = 1.38583... is the precise distance between the double cube's spherical boundary and the Delian constant. The Delian problem appears to operate in the same gap — this connection deserves formal attention.

Layer 5 — The 1/7 decimal cycle as internal periodicity

The double cube's vertex count encodes 7 as a cyclic structure — not just as an absent lattice point.

The two corner counts, 16 (uncoupled) and 12 (coupled), share a common factor: 48 is the least common multiple of both. Scaling each by 3 gives the natural pair 48 and 36, which preserve the system's ratio 4:3. When both are divided by the saddle point 7/4:

36 ÷ (7/4) = 144/7 = 20 + 4/7
48 ÷ (7/4) = 192/7 = 27 + 3/7

The fractional remainders are 4/7 and 3/7. In decimal, these are mirror rotations within the same cycle:

4/7 = 0.571428 571428…
3/7 = 0.428571 428571…

Both are rotations of 142857 — the repeating cycle of 1/7. They sum to exactly 1 (a closed cycle), and their numerators satisfy:

4 + 3 = 7     4 × 3 = 12

The sum recovers the combinator. The product recovers the coupled vertex count. The saddle point 7/4, when applied to the system's own scaling numbers, unfolds 7 from the inside — not as a missing corner, but as a period. This is precisely what Layer 3 anticipated: √7 requires a cyclic, temporal dimension. Here it is, encoded in the decimal structure of the vertex arithmetic.

Here's where the real magic starts — if we take this question seriously, we may propose one major assumption considering the mathematical proof: the double cube, bound by √5 as its dynamic connector, points towards a closed system. √7, in this scenario, is not a stranger to this cubic system (as layer 3 in the mathematical proofs suggests), creating an escaping diagonal, but might actually curve inside the system.

The halfpoint of return

By treating √7 as an anomalous 4D object in a 3D lattice, and considering the position of its tangent (as proof Layer 3b indicates), it makes sense to find its mathematical midpoint as well as its geometric position. By halving √7 and then squaring the result, we get:

(√7 / 2)² = 7/4 = 1.75

This is the same value as the tangential proof. 7/4 seems to be a valid candidate for the geometric signature of the curvature in two ways — both geometrically derived and by pure mathematics. It hints at 7/4 being a candidate saddle point at the interface between cubic and spherical symmetry in the double cube — where these two geometries can negotiate without either dominating. √7's value also places it in the second cube.

As the numbers and examples show, assuming √7 as part of the double cube is more than a fair assumption.

Identifying a delta that makes a difference

While (√6/2)² = 6/4 = 1.500 turns out geometrically predictable, (√7/2)² = 7/4 = 1.750 shows its anomalous nature. The value 7/4 exceeds √3 by exactly 0.018. Placing itself this close to the half-point of the double cube when treated as both a linear and a geometric number at once makes for a compelling argument of curvature through this value as a saddle point. In this sense, the delta area between √3 and 7/4 might be considered an area of interconnection, hinging the two cubes through the introduced curve.

√7, given the assumption of existing within the double cube, is the one that will not be squared into a corner. Instead it literally wants to play ball. The √7 diagonal traverses space and ends up roaming the cubes. It's the negotiator between the two regimes.
The two binders

So here we are.

We have two separate cubes and two numbers that bind them together: √5 and √7.

√5

The dynamic, geometric one. It traverses the two cubes diagonally and tells us that, when combined, a new property emerges — a structural communality that presents us with the minimal integer lattice construction that contains φ as an internal geometric ratio.

√7

The odd one. It tells us that negotiating the cyclical geometry that √5 hints at involves more than the corners. It needs the space. And it does so by hinting about the fact that the double cube hides a spherical, periodic element.

Two cubes, one system

The double cube is a peculiar object in this way — it is a geometric paradox. It allows us to see numbers in their true two-faced properties: the cyclic/periodic and the structural/geometric.

This tension between these two modes is built into the structure itself:

  1. The double cube consists of two similar cubes.
  2. Each cube has eight corners — two cubes give 16 corners.
  3. When they share a face, as in the double cube, they also become a unit and lose 4 corners — leaving 12.
  4. The double cube is simultaneously 16 and 12 corners. Both a regular, static lattice and a dynamic space.

At first glance this might seem contradictory. But it is not a contradiction — it is the system's own way of holding two regimes at once.

The spherical element that the √7 tangent revealed might not just be a geometric curiosity. It has a precise half expression given its place in the double cube, and names the candidate saddle point in the system, as mentioned before:

(√7 / 2)² = 7/4 = 1.75

Simply put: √7 is the geometry. 7/4 is what it does.

The corner count

The cube being both 16 and 12 corners simultaneously — combined with the saddle point, produces two exact identities:

(7/4) + √(1/16) = 2

The saddle point plus the shared face's unit returns exactly the double cube's full length — √4 = 2.

(7/4) ÷ √(1/16) = 7

The saddle point divided by the shared face's unit returns exactly the combinator.

These are not coincidences. √(1/16) = 1/4 is the structural complement to the saddle point — together they complete the double cube's length (7/4 + 1/4 = 2) and recover the anomaly (7/4 ÷ 1/4 = 7).

The double cube has 12 vertices — 16 minus the 4 shared at the joining face. If the saddle point 7/4 is a property of the whole system, it should scale with the vertex count:

7/4 × 12 = 21 = 3 × 7

The factor 3 is the spherical content — three spheres inscribed along the double cube's length (½ + 1 + ½). The factor 7 is the combinator. The product 21 is where cubic topology and spherical geometry meet as an exact integer.

Proposition: The dynamic side of the double cube

Definition 1. Let a double cube be two unit cubes linked along one shared face. The double cube has 2×8 − 4 = 12 corners, where 4 shared corners are subtracted.

Definition 2. Let the saddle point S be defined as S = (√7/2)² = 7/4, where √7 is the combinator — established as forbidden by Legendre's three-square theorem.

Lemma. For n linked unit cubes, the number of corners is 4(n+1), and the product with the saddle point is:

4(n+1) · 7/4 = 7(n+1)

Corollary for n = 2:

12 · 7/4 = 21 = 3 × 7

Four independent convergences on 21:

1. √21 = √(1² + 2² + 4²) — space diagonal of a 1×2×4 rectangle, where 1, 2, 4 are the first three powers of 2.

2. 7π/21 = π/3 = 60° exactly — base angle of hexagonal crystallography, and the phase angle per spherical segment when a curve winds around the three spheres (½ + 1 + ½) of the double cube. Six such segments complete a full phase of 2π.

On the missing axiom

The weighting of corners by 7/4 is geometrically motivated but not formally proved. However, a candidate axiom emerges from the spherical structure of the double cube: a helix winding around the three inscribed spheres (½ + 1 + ½) traverses exactly 2π over the double cube's full length — one complete phase per two cubic units. This gives π per unit length, and π/3 per spherical segment. The connection between cubic topology and spherical weighting may be precisely this phase relationship.

Geometric observation
When 7/4 is combined with the 1/16 factor — the shared face factor of a tesselating double cube — it completes the double cube's full length: (7/4) + √(1/16) = 2 = √4. At the same time, the saddle point 7/4 avoids the lattice, hinting at a perturbative nature. This becomes visible when the same operation is applied with the 1/12 factor: (7/4) + √(1/12) ≈ 2.0386 — exceeding the double cube's boundary by a small but non-zero amount. The double cube is structurally closed but dynamically open: it can only close itself with the structural complement 1/16, not with the negotiating gap 1/12.
The modest double cube may turn out to deliver a great punch for its size and shape. Assuming √7 is contained in the construction, it gives us an operating system where the cyclical and geometric domains meet.

This might point to something even deeper: do we see the possible shape of a hypothesis? Every number carries both these properties at the same time — factorisation uncovers its geometrical identity, while the cyclical uncovers a cumulative position.

Synthesis

A closing remark and a new perspective.

The double cube carries two axes: a linear, cubic extent — the x-axis, the direction along which the two cubes are joined — and a spherical, curved contribution — the y-axis, the direction in which √7 departs from the lattice.

In each of the system's characteristic fractions, these two axes appear as numerator and denominator.

7/4 — the spherical element √7 (numerator) curves over the double cube's squared length 2² = 4 (denominator). This is the saddle point: the projection of the √7 sphere onto the cubic axis, derived exactly from the tangent point construction. Reading: spherical over cubic.

3/4 — the spatial remainder from the saddle point decomposition 7/4 = 1 + 3/4. The numerator corresponds to the three spheres inscribed in the double cube (½ + 1 + ½). The denominator is the squared length of the double cube (2² = 4) — the same structural unit that anchors 7/4. Reading: spherical content over cubic extent.

2/3 — two cubic units (numerator) over three spherical units (denominator). Here the double cube itself becomes the unit: two cubes constitute the structure, three spheres constitute the periodicity. The same relationship, read from the opposite direction. Reading: cubic over spherical.

These are not three separate observations. They are two directions of the same geometric relationship — spherical over cubic (7/4 and 3/4) and cubic over spherical (2/3). The difference between them is:

3/4 − 2/3 = 1/12

The interval 1/12 is what remains when the two readings — structure looking at periodicity, and periodicity looking at structure — fail to agree exactly. It is the negotiating remainder: the irreducible gap between the cubic and the spherical, expressed as a single fraction.

The threshold

A level up.

While the double cube remains the most basic expression for how structure and dynamics might be symmetrically linked, the continuation of such a system seems to point towards an iterating development.

In the double cube, √7 acts as the dynamic operator within the structural framework of √1 through √6. It does so through (√7/2)² = 1.75 — indicating a series of scaling primes of the form 0.75 + n, where 0.75 itself is the base value given by (√3/2)² — the unit cube's space diagonal.

This remains to be explored.

The double cube generates exact boundary conditions (2/3, 3/4, 1/12) and a saddle point (7/4). If these are real geometric constraints, they should be visible in physical systems built on periodicity. The periodic table is the most precisely known such system.
The evidence

Where the geometry becomes visible.

The periodic table of elements is one of the places where this operating system manifests in the physical world. The same double cube geometry — the same √3, √5 and √7 — organises the elements, predicts their chemical character, and places the resonance curve exactly where the geometry says it must be.
