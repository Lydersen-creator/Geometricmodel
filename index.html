The periodic table is one of the most studied objects in science. And yet it contains a geometric structure that has gone largely unnoticed — not because it is hidden, but because it only becomes visible in the wide format.

The observation

Something hidden in plain sight

Most versions of the periodic table fold the lanthanides and actinides away. The wide format lays everything out in 32 columns. When it does, a curve appears — at least for the shape-inclined among us.

If we follow the curve, two circles fill its inflections naturally. This is where the connections start to line up — because these two circles have a radius ratio of √3.

This model begins as a hand-drawn vector construction, not a computed fit. The curve is drawn by eye through the visual core of the wide-format table. The circles that fill its inflections are placed by hand.

Which makes the following observation more striking, not less. The two circles are not equal. The larger, C1, sits above the table. The smaller, C2, has its centre at lead — Pb, atomic number 82, the heaviest stable element and a nuclear magic number. That placement was not chosen. It followed from the geometry.

Their radius ratio R1/R2 is so close to √3 that in a computed version of this construction, we expect the ratio to be exactly √3.

The double cube

An overlap that lands somewhere

The √3 ratio between C1 and C2, combined with the long rectangular form of the wide-format table, geometrically suggested a natural next step: a double cube. The right-hand reach of C1 sits close to the geometric centre of the table, making it a natural boundary for the first cube. Two cubes of that size, placed side by side, would span the full table.

They did not tessellate. The geometric intuition appeared to be falsified.

Then the overlap was measured.

The two cubes overlap by a small amount — δ = 14 units against a cube side of 327. That overlap lands precisely at the boundary between group 2 and group 3 — between the s-block and the d-block, between the elements that fill the left side of the resonance curve and the elements that begin the straight segment.

In this light, the almost tessellating cubes might be evaluated as a signal and not a failure.

The side length 327 follows from the chosen cell size of 20 mm. The ratio δ/side = 14/327 is scale-independent. The system is dimensionless.

The use of a double cube rather than a single or triple cube was not arbitrary. A single cube does not contain √7 — the diagonal that signals the spherical element. A triple cube produces diagonals √8 = 2√2 and √9 = 3, which are multiples of existing diagonals rather than new geometric qualities.

√7 does not traverse beyond the double cube. It is defined by the double cube's full extent — the distance that requires both the full length and both cubes to exist simultaneously. The double cube is the minimal sufficient structure: the smallest system that contains √7 and therefore the smallest system that can carry both the structural and dynamic sides of the geometry.

The missing root

√7 and the spherical contribution

A double cube is widely recognised as home to the diagonal lines described by the square roots of 1 through 6, addressing the corners of the cubes. However, √7 belongs to the curvature — it needs both cubes to exist, and shows us that the system might also have a dynamic side.

Recognising √7 as a natural part of a two-cube system, its dynamic expression might be quantised as:

(√7 / 2)² = 7/4 = 1.75

√7 is the geometry. 7/4 is its dynamic expression.

Now connect this to delta. The percolation interval — the channel width — is 1/12. And:

7/4 × 12 = 21 = 3 × 7

The overlapping double cube delta is 14 = 2 × 7. The full percolation space is 21 = 3 × 7. It seems these are not separate instances, but the same geometric fact expressed at three different scales.

The percolation interval 1/12 is not imported from biology. It is derived from the double cube's own diagonal structure.

The structural cube contributes the ratio of its face diagonal to its space diagonal:

√2 / √3

The dynamic cube contributes the ratio of its space diagonal to the double cube's full length:

√3 / √4 = √3 / 2

When squared, these ratios produce the percolation boundary conditions:

(√2 / √3)² = 2/3
(√3 / √4)² = 3/4

The percolation interval is the difference between them:

3/4 − 2/3 = 1/12

√7 is the coupling term that requires both cubes simultaneously. Without it, the structural and dynamic sides are independent. With it, the interval 1/12 becomes geometrically necessary.

Predictions

What the geometry predicts

A geometric model is only as interesting as what it predicts. The following observations were not derived from chemical knowledge — they follow from the position of elements relative to C1, C2 and the resonance curve. The chemistry was checked afterwards. In each case, the chemical fact was already known. What the model offers is an independent geometric derivation of known results — which is a different kind of evidence.

Confirmed

Fe, Ru and Os as universal mediators

The geometry places these elements at the crossing point between the stable boundary interior and the open percolation zone. The prediction: they should be the most chemically universal bonders in the transition metals. Fe, Ru and Os are among the most catalytically versatile elements known. Iron is the centre of haemoglobin. The geometry did not need to know this.

Confirmed

Be and Mg — the diagonal anomaly

The resonance curve passes through beryllium and magnesium. The prediction: they should behave anomalously relative to the rest of group 2 — more covalently, resembling their diagonal neighbours more than their vertical group neighbours. This is the diagonal rule — one of the known anomalies of the periodic table. The model placed Be and Mg on the curve independently.

Confirmed

Pb as a potential minimum

C2 has its centre at lead. In the field geometry, C2 is the lower boundary condition. Lead has atomic number 82, a nuclear magic number. It is the heaviest stable element. That the geometry places C2 at Pb is either a remarkable coincidence or a signal worth following.

Type 1 — Ti, Zr, Hf: These sit inside the C1 boundary. The field is maximally stable here; there is no gradient driving bond formation. Their inertness is structural. Confirmed: Ti, Zr and Hf are among the most corrosion-resistant transition metals, used in implants and nuclear cladding.

Type 2 — heavy halogens, noble gases: These sit in the peripheral zone where the harmonic series approaches its 7/4 threshold. Their inertness is dynamic: the system is closing one period and beginning the next. Noble gases are fixed points of the geometric transformation. Two different geometric mechanisms. Two different chemical characters.

The atomic number ratio (Z_Yb − Z_Sc) / (Z_Rn − Z_H) = 49/85 = 0.57647 lies within 0.00088 of the percolation threshold 1/√3 = 0.57735.

The numerator counts the span from the first d-block element to the last lanthanide. The denominator counts the full span of the table through period 6. This is a counting statement about the table itself — no external constants are involved. Whether this proximity is structural or coincidental is not known. It is noted here as a convergence, not a conclusion.

A bridge to biology

Kleibers law

In 1932, Max Kleiber observed that the metabolic rate of living organisms scales as M^(3/4) across all scales — from bacteria to whales. The naive geometric prediction from surface-to-volume scaling is M^(2/3). The difference between them is:

3/4 − 2/3 = 1/12

The double cube generates this interval exactly from its own diagonal structure. The structural cube contributes (√2/√3)² = 2/3. The dynamic cube contributes (√3/√4)² = 3/4. The interval between them is 1/12.

The proposed reading: biological metabolic scaling operates at the upper boundary condition of the double cube's percolation channel. The exponent 3/4 is the squared diagonal ratio of the dynamic cube — the same geometric object that defines the dynamic axis of the periodic table.

This is proposed as a model, not asserted as a result. The geometric derivation of 2/3, 3/4 and 1/12 from the double cube is exact. The connection to Kleibers law is structurally compelling — but a formal derivation has not yet been produced. That derivation is an open problem.

What can be said is this: the double cube gives us 1/12. Kleibers law requires 1/12. That is not nothing.

Status

Where this leads — and where it does not

This is a geometric observation, not a completed theory. It began with a curve drawn by hand through a table that most scientists encounter in a different format. It produced a ratio, a structure and a set of correspondences that were not anticipated at the outset.

We are aware that the results may appear too tidy. Three chemical predictions confirmed by known chemistry. A geometric interval that matches the gap in Kleibers law exactly. These correspondences invite scepticism — and that scepticism is reasonable.

What we can say is that each step in the construction follows from the previous one without free parameters. The curve is drawn. The circles emerge. The ratio is measured. The double cube follows. The overlap is what it is. Nothing is fitted to produce a desired result.

The geometry of C1 and C2 generates the framework within which molecular bond angles can be located — the system contains the t-point for bond angle geometry. But the algorithm for placing ligands has not been found.

The lanthanides and actinides have not been placed within the percolation framework in a way that generates predictions.

The formal derivation of the Laplace solution between C1 and C2 has not been completed. The model points toward it. The calculation remains to be done.

The connection to Kleibers law has not been formally derived. The geometric interval matches. The formal derivation is an open problem.

These are not weaknesses to be apologised for. They are the open problems that make the model worth pursuing.

The model is offered here as a proposal — precise enough to be falsified, open enough to invite collaboration. If the geometric derivations are wrong, they can be shown to be wrong. If they are right, the implications will take time to work through properly.

That seems like the right place to leave it.
