A geometric exploration

Negotiating numbers.

How space may coordinate the values of geometric and accumulating numbers.

Every number has two faces: the geometric/structural and the cumulative/linear — we use the same numbers to describe both coordinates and sums. These two very different domains, normally treated as separate phenomena, are calculable with the same system consisting of 10 numbers in infinite combinations: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 0.

Although the system has served us well since the beginning of calculation, the question remains:

Are the dual-natured properties of numbers related in a certain way?
Is there a geometry that coordinates them?
The structure

Meet the double cube.

The structure that corners the numbers.

The double cube has been used since ancient times to explain the geometric properties of numbers. It is a visual and educational masterpiece. But it might be more than that.

This page proposes that the double cube — two unit cubes joined along a shared face — constitutes a complete geometric system in which structure and dynamics are symmetrically coupled through the introduction of √7 as an integral part of the double cube.

By introducing √7, the structure becomes a system that can be shown to contain characteristic fractions (7/4, 3/4, 2/3, 1/12, 1/7) that emerge from a single geometric object through five converging arguments.

√7 is therefore the system's combinator — negotiating between the double cube's two regimes, the cyclical and the structural. It does so despite being forbidden from the integer lattice by Legendre's three-square theorem, entering the system as both a saddle point and a dynamic operator — the same value described algebraically and geometrically.

What follows is a layered argument for this claim.

Ordinary and extraordinary

The cornered numbers — and the presence of φ.

A single cube shows us √1, √2 and √3 — edge, face diagonal, space diagonal. Add a second cube sharing a face, and something interesting happens. √4 and √6 are natural doublings of what we already know. As it seems, the second cube is not just a repetition of the single cube. √5 tells us that something new emerges when the two seemingly similar structures are combined — as tessellating cubes.

√4 and √6

Natural doublings of √2 and √3. Structural. Predictable. The double cube's space diagonals — still playing by the rules from the first cube.

√5

The dynamic connector. Traverses both cubes. Cannot exist in a single cube alone. Tells us the double cube also represents something cyclical. And it carries a familiar constant: φ = (1 + √5)/2 — the golden ratio, already present in the system, not imported from outside.

φ

The double cube is the minimal integer lattice construction that contains φ as an internal geometric ratio. The right triangle with sides 1, 2 and √5 is inscribed in the double cube's own face — and φ = (1 + √5)/2 is the ratio of these three lengths: the short side plus the hypotenuse, divided by the long side.

Written in the system's own terms, this becomes:

φ = (√1 + √5) / √4

The first root, the dynamic connector, and the full length of the double cube — φ emerges directly from the system's own square roots.

The invisible turns visible

The one missing in action.

When studying the double cube, something else peculiar turns up: adding a third cube will not create the same effect; you won't get another √5 factor. Instead you end up with a cleaner, more easily divisible √10 corner, echoing √2.

And before we even go so far as to thinking of adding another cube:
What about √7? Does it even belong in this cubic setting?

If we take this question seriously, we may propose a single working assumption: √7, though excluded from the integer lattice, belongs geometrically to the double cube's system.

The cube effectively codes √1 to √3, and adding a tessellating cube expands this to √1 to √6. These are all natural cubic proportions. √7 is a combinator: √(2² + (√3)²) = √7. It is constructable over both regimes at the same time as it is not part of the convex outer shell of the double cube. The tangent from the √7 sphere to the √6 surface is √(7−6) = √1 = 1 exactly.

The double cube, historically, has not been regarded as a system of numbers. That may be why the question about which square roots it produces — and more pressing, why √7 sits outside the system — seems not to have been asked formally.

Layer 1 — Exhaustive verification

All 12 corners of the 2×1×1 double cube have coordinates (x,y,z) where x∈{0,1,2}, y∈{0,1}, z∈{0,1}. The squared distances x²+y²+z² from the origin — and between all corner pairs — produce exactly the set {0,1,2,3,4,5,6}. No pair of corners produces a squared distance of 7. This is not an axiom. It is a finite, exhaustive proof.

Layer 2 — Legendre's three-square theorem (1798)

A positive integer n can be written as the sum of three squares of integers if and only if n is not of the form 4ᵃ(8b+7). The number 7 satisfies this exactly: 7 = 4⁰(8·0+7). It is the simplest and smallest member of this infinite family of forbidden values — {7, 15, 23, 28, 31, ...}. This is a proved theorem. √7 is absent from the double cube's lattice by mathematical necessity.

Layer 3 — Lagrange's four-square theorem

To realise 7 as a sum of integer squares, four terms are required: 7 = 2²+1²+1²+1². Geometrically, this means √7 requires a fourth spatial dimension to exist as a lattice point. In the context of the double cube, this fourth degree of freedom is interpreted as periodicity: a temporal or cyclic dimension that complements the three spatial ones.

The first argument

Argument 1: The tangent proof.

The double cube has length √4 = 2. Working in the squared metric — consistent with Legendre's theorem, which establishes that √7 is only realisable through a four-term sum of squares — the external point is placed at (√4)² = 4. Draw a circle of radius √7 centred at the origin. Place an external point P = (4, 0). The tangent length is exact:

|PT| = √(16 − 7) = √9 = 3

The x-coordinate of the tangent point:

Tₓ = r² / |OP| = 7 / 4 = 1.75

The saddle point is not placed by assumption — it is where the tangent from the double cube's squared length meets the √7 sphere. This is a geometric derivation: 7/4 emerges from the construction itself.

The second argument

Argument 2: The midpoint proof.

Independently of the tangent construction, the same value appears through pure arithmetic. By halving √7 and squaring the result:

(√7 / 2)² = 7/4 = 1.75

This is the same value as Argument 1. That two independent routes — one geometric, one arithmetic — converge on precisely 7/4 establishes it as the saddle point: the value at which the √7 sphere and the double cube's own geometry meet. It is not assumed. It is derived twice.

Interlude

Two in One.

Before continuing, let's pause for a second and absorb what these first two arguments actually mean. We have two separate cubes joined in a special relationship by two numbers: √5 and √7.

√5

The dynamic, geometric one. It traverses the two cubes diagonally and tells us that, when combined, a new property emerges — a structural communality that presents us with the minimal integer lattice construction that contains φ as an internal geometric ratio.

√7

The odd one. It tells us that negotiating the cyclical geometry that √5 hints at involves more than the corners. It needs the space. And it does so by hinting about the fact that the double cube hides a spherical, periodic element.

The double cube is a geometric nexus — it allows us to see numbers in their true dual-natured properties: the cyclic/periodic and the structural/geometric. Simply put: √7 is the geometry. 7/4 is what it does.

The modest double cube may turn out to deliver a great punch for its size and shape. Assuming √7 is contained in the construction, it gives us an operating system where the cyclical and geometric domains meet.
The third argument

Argument 3: The corner count.

The double cube is simultaneously two things: a structure of two separate cubes, each with 8 corners — giving 16 in total — and a coupled system where the shared face removes 4 of those corners, leaving 12. These are not two different objects. They are two readings of the same object: one structural, one dynamic.

The saddle point 7/4 responds to both readings precisely. Combined with the shared face's unit √(1/16) = 1/4, it produces two exact identities:

(7/4) + √(1/16) = 2

The saddle point plus the shared face's unit returns exactly the double cube's full length — √4 = 2.

(7/4) ÷ √(1/16) = 7

The saddle point divided by the shared face's unit returns exactly the combinator.

√(1/16) = 1/4 is not an arbitrary factor — it is the geometric unit of the shared face itself. One face of a unit cube has area 1, divided by 16 corners gives 1/16. The shared face is precisely the interface where the structural and dynamic readings diverge: it is present in one reading and absent in the other. That the saddle point both completes the double cube's length and recovers the combinator through this single factor suggests that 7/4 is not just a property of the system — it is sensitive to the system's own internal boundary.

16 corners — two separate cubes

8 + 8 = 16

12 corners — joined double cube, with one shared face

8 + 8 − 4 = 12

The fourth argument

Argument 4: The connecting sequence.

As we have seen, the double cube may be understood as two systems operating simultaneously. A structural system of two separate but tessellating cubes, and a dynamic system of two combined cubes sharing a face.

These two properties are tied in a proportional relationship with deep connections to the interplay between structure and dynamics. The numbers that emerge are striking:

(16 − 12) / (16 + 12) = 4/28 = 1/7

1/7 is one of the most fascinating relationships in mathematics, producing the repeating decimal sequence 0.142857142857... — a closed, self-returning cycle of six digits. That this sequence emerges directly from the double cube's own corner arithmetic provides grounds for further investigation.

The 12 and 16 corner counts meet in a common denominator when scaled by 3: 3 × 12 = 36 and 3 × 16 = 48. The number 48 is the least common multiple of both — the first number into which both 12 and 16 divide exactly.

When both are divided by the saddle point 7/4:

36 ÷ (7/4) = 144/7 = 20 + 4/7
48 ÷ (7/4) = 192/7 = 27 + 3/7

The fractional remainders — 4/7 and 3/7 — are mirror rotations within the same cycle:

4/7  = 0.5 7 1 4 2 8  5 7 1 4 2 8  5 7 1 4 2 8…
3/7  = 0.4 2 8 5 7 1  4 2 8 5 7 1  4 2 8 5 7 1…
4/7 + 3/7 = 1    4 + 3 = 7    4 × 3 = 12
They sum to exactly 1, and their numerators satisfy 4 + 3 = 7 and 4 × 3 = 12. The saddle point 7/4, when applied to the system's scaled corner numbers, unfolds the 1/7 sequence from within the double cube's own arithmetic.

The two residuals are not arbitrary: 4 = 2² is the squared length of the double cube, and 3 is the spherical content (½ + 1 + ½). The saddle point 7/4, as divisor, separates the system's cubic and spherical signatures into two distinct positions within the 1/7 cycle.

Three relationships now stand together:

1. φ is present in the double cube through its root expression: φ = (√1 + √5) / √4 — the sum of the system's first and dynamic endpoints, divided by its full length.

2. √7 belongs in the double cube through its tangent point at 7/4 — the precise location where the √7 sphere touches the double cube's geometry, derived from the tangent construction at P = (4, 0).

3. 1/7 emerges from the double cube's own corner relationship: (16 − 12) / (16 + 12) = 1/7.

1/7 does not merely appear in the double cube. It is locked to it — a consequence of φ and √4 existing in the same geometric system.

The fifth argument

Argument 5: Curving the structure.

5.1 — The dynamic operator

In the double cube, √7 acts as the dynamic operator within the structural framework of √1 through √6. It does so through (√7/2)² = 1.75 — the saddle point at which the system's cubic and spherical geometries meet without either dominating.

The broader implications of √7 as a scaling operator — and its possible connections to physical periodicity — are explored in Possible Implications.

The scaling value 1.75 = 7/4 is not only an arithmetic property — it is the saddle point through which the dynamic curve passes. The operator and the curve are the same phenomenon: one described algebraically, the other geometrically.

5.2 — The sinusoidal curve

The dynamic curve winds through the double cube as a single sinusoidal phase — from the lower apex of the left half-sphere, through the upper apex of the central sphere, to the lower apex of the right half-sphere. This is one complete period, inscribed exactly within the geometry.

5.3 — Tessellation and the 3×7 product

The half-spheres at each end have radius ½ — exactly matching the half-sphere of the adjacent double cube in an orthogonal tessellation. The saddle point 7/4 is defined relative to the double cube's internal geometry and is therefore translationally invariant: the same saddle point applies in each tessellating unit. The product 7/4 × 12 = 21 = 3 × 7 is not a local result — it is a structural property of the entire tessellating system.

The double cube has 12 vertices — 16 minus the 4 shared at the joining face. The saddle point 7/4, as a property of the whole system, scales with this vertex count:

7/4 × 12 = 21 = 3 × 7

The factor 3 is the spherical content — three spheres inscribed along the double cube's length (½ + 1 + ½). The factor 7 is the combinator. The product 21 is where cubic topology and spherical geometry meet as an exact integer.

5.4 — The Banach space framework

The lattice geometry of the double cube exhibits the properties of a Banach space with a non-Euclidean norm: a well-defined norm, completeness, and a geometry in which geodesic and circular paths coincide. In this space, the natural circle constant is π = 2 — consistent with the structure's inherent 2-fold rotational symmetry. This places the double cube's geometry within an established mathematical framework, without requiring it.

Proposition: The dynamic side of the double cube

Definition 1. Let a double cube be two unit cubes linked along one shared face. The double cube has 2×8 − 4 = 12 corners, where 4 shared corners are subtracted.

Definition 2. Let the saddle point S be defined as S = (√7/2)² = 7/4, where √7 is the combinator — established as forbidden by Legendre's three-square theorem.

Lemma. For n linked unit cubes, the number of corners is 4(n+1), and the product with the saddle point is:

4(n+1) · 7/4 = 7(n+1)

Corollary for n = 2:

12 · 7/4 = 21 = 3 × 7

Two independent convergences on 21:

1. √21 = √(1² + 2² + 4²) — space diagonal of a 1×2×4 rectangle, where 1, 2, 4 are the first three powers of 2.

2. 7π/21 = π/3 = 60° exactly — base angle of hexagonal crystallography, and the phase angle per spherical segment when a curve winds around the three spheres (½ + 1 + ½) of the double cube. Six such segments complete a full phase of 2π.

On the missing axiom

The weighting of corners by 7/4 is geometrically motivated but not formally proved. However, a candidate axiom emerges from the spherical structure of the double cube: a helix winding around the three inscribed spheres (½ + 1 + ½) traverses exactly 2π over the double cube's full length — one complete phase per two cubic units. This gives π per unit length, and π/3 per spherical segment. The connection between cubic topology and spherical weighting may be precisely this phase relationship.

Interlude

Delta of connectivity.

While (√6/2)² = 6/4 = 1.500 turns out geometrically predictable, (√7/2)² = 7/4 = 1.750 shows its anomalous nature. The value 7/4 exceeds √3 by exactly 0.018. Placing itself this close to the half-point of the double cube when treated as both a linear and a geometric number at once makes for a compelling argument of curvature through this value as a saddle point. In this sense, the delta area between √3 and 7/4 might be considered an area of interconnection, hinging the two cubes through the introduced curve.

The numbers

What the double cube gives us.

Five arguments, five fractions, one system.

The five arguments developed above each approach the double cube from a different direction — tangent geometry, arithmetic midpoint, corner topology, connecting sequence, and spherical inscription. They converge on the same set of characteristic fractions. This section collects what they jointly produce.

The double cube carries two axes: a linear, cubic extent and a spherical, curved contribution. In each of the system's characteristic fractions, these two axes appear as numerator and denominator — and their difference names the irreducible gap between them.

7/4 — the spherical element √7 (numerator) curves over the double cube's squared length 2² = 4 (denominator). This is the saddle point: the projection of the √7 sphere onto the cubic axis, derived exactly from the tangent point construction. Reading: spherical over cubic.

3/4 — the spatial remainder from the saddle point decomposition 7/4 = 1 + 3/4. The numerator corresponds to the three spheres inscribed in the double cube (½ + 1 + ½). The denominator is the squared length of the double cube (2² = 4) — the same structural unit that anchors 7/4. Reading: spherical content over cubic extent.

2/3 — two cubic units (numerator) over three spherical units (denominator). Here the double cube itself becomes the unit: two cubes constitute the structure, three spheres constitute the periodicity. The same relationship, read from the opposite direction. Reading: cubic over spherical.

These are not three separate observations. They are two directions of the same geometric relationship — spherical over cubic (7/4 and 3/4) and cubic over spherical (2/3). Their consequences follow directly.

1/12 — the difference between the two readings: 3/4 − 2/3. It is the negotiating remainder: the irreducible gap between the cubic and the spherical, expressed as a single fraction.

3/4 − 2/3 = 1/12

1/7 — the ratio of difference to sum in the double cube's two corner counts: (16 − 12) / (16 + 12). The combinator appears not as a missing corner, but as a period — a return encoded in the system's own arithmetic.

(16 − 12) / (16 + 12) = 4/28 = 1/7
The double cube is a geometric nexus. It allows us to see numbers in their true dual-natured properties working purposefully together: the cyclic/periodic and the structural/geometric. It is a system connecting these domains numerically, showing an exact relation. This convergence is not arbitrary. It is a geometric consequence of a number-theoretic constraint: 7 cannot be realised as a diagonal in any three-dimensional integer lattice. The double cube is the largest structure that exists beneath that ceiling — and the only asymmetric one.
Beyond the double cube

Where the geometry might become visible.

The double cube generates exact boundary conditions (2/3, 3/4, 1/12), a saddle point (7/4) and a symmetry (1/7). If these are real geometric constraints, they should be detectable in physical systems built on periodicity. The periodic table is the most precisely known such system. Can we see the double cube manifested there?
